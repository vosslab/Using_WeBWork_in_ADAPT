Experimental design and controls are central to biology and are well-suited to multiple choice and multiple answer items when the prompt is concrete. Reliable grading depends on clear definitions of what a "control" is in the scenario.

Good fits for this pattern

  • Pick the best control for a stated confound (MC).
  • Select which design changes fix a bias (MA).
  • Match common controls to their purpose (MATCH).

What breaks in practice

  • Vague prompts: "What is the control?" without describing the confound.
  • Multiple plausible controls when the criterion is not stated.
  • Items become opinion questions unless the prompt anchors the decision to a specific threat to validity.

Prompt outline

Story: treatment vs vehicle in a cell assay.
Task: which control isolates the effect of the active compound?
